\begin{abstract}
Robot foundation policies are usually trained and evaluated as smooth input-output maps, yet contact-rich manipulation is full of non-smooth events: impacts, stick-slip transitions, unilateral contact activation, jamming, release, and support changes. This paper rebuilds Paper 109 around a falsifiable mechanism: a policy should audit contact-mode boundaries before interpolating through them. We implement a local benchmark with 5 contact-rich task families, 7 non-smooth regimes, 5 deployment splits, 9 methods, 7 paired seeds, and 84 episodes per group. Under combined contact shock, the proposed contact-mode boundary atlas obtains $0.540 \pm 0.004$ success versus $0.463 \pm 0.007$ for the strongest non-oracle baseline, complementarity residual planning. It improves contact-mode F1 from $0.495$ to $0.630$, reduces unsafe impulse from $0.079$ to $0.064$, reduces jam rate from $0.112$ to $0.092$, reduces slip overshoot from $0.076$ to $0.057$, and wins 7/7 paired seeds. Ablations show that mode classification, complementarity residuals, impulse guards, stick-slip hysteresis, and diagnostic probes each matter. The v4.1 audit expands stress-sweep evidence to task/regime/seed granularity and records eight failure cases. The terminal decision is \textbf{STRONG\_REVISE}: the local evidence supports the mechanism, but the paper is not ICLR-main-ready without real robot or independent high-fidelity validation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}
Contact-rich manipulation breaks the smoothness assumptions that many learned policy abstractions inherit. A gripper approaching a peg can cross from free motion to impact; a drawer can switch between stick and slip; a cable can jam; a tool can release stored elastic energy. These are not merely difficult examples. They are mode changes where smooth interpolation can produce physically invalid actions.

Classical mechanics already studies non-smooth contact through unilateral constraints, impacts, friction, and complementarity-style formulations \citep{moreau1999nonsmooth,stewart1996implicit,anitescu1997formulating}. Modern robot simulators and contact-learning methods also expose contact discontinuities \citep{todorov2012mujoco,pfrommer2020contactnets}. Meanwhile, large learned robot policies and visuomotor policies show that broad behavior can be learned from data \citep{chi2023diffusion,brohan2022rt1,brohan2023rt2}. The question here is narrower: when a learned policy is asked to act near a non-smooth contact boundary, does an explicit contact-mode audit help?

\section{Method sketch}
The proposed method, \texttt{proposed\_contact\_mode\_boundary\_atlas}, is not a new contact solver. It is a policy-auditing layer that scores whether a candidate action crosses a physically meaningful contact-mode boundary.

The audit uses five components:
\begin{itemize}
\item a contact-mode classifier over free, touch, stick, slip, jam, release, and impact modes;
\item a complementarity-residual score for physically impossible smooth predictions;
\item an impulse-budget guard near impact onset and release snap;
\item stick-slip hysteresis memory to avoid oscillating across incompatible modes;
\item diagnostic micro-probes for ambiguous contact states.
\end{itemize}

The policy chooses or rejects actions using these scores. The implementation is an executable local benchmark rather than a trained robot foundation model, which is why the final decision remains strong-revise rather than submission-ready.

\section{Benchmark}
The benchmark is generated by \texttt{src/run\_experiment.py}. It emits seed-level CSVs, aggregate metrics, paired tests, stress sweeps with task/regime/seed detail, ablations, failure cases, figures, and LaTeX tables.

\paragraph{Tasks.}
The five task families are peg insertion, drawer opening, cable routing, tool levering, and mobile pushing.

\paragraph{Regimes.}
The seven non-smooth regimes are nominal contact, impact onset, stick-slip transition, unilateral lift-off, contact jamming, release snap, and combined contact shock.

\paragraph{Splits.}
The deployment splits are nominal, seen shift, unseen geometry, unseen friction, and combined stress.

\paragraph{Methods.}
The comparison set includes no-contact behavior cloning, domain randomization, diffusion-policy surrogate, robotics-transformer surrogate, ensemble disagreement planning, conformal risk filtering, complementarity residual planning, the proposed contact-mode boundary atlas, and an oracle contact-mode supervisor.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
We report task success, contact-mode F1, boundary error, unsafe impulse rate, jam rate, slip overshoot, intervention cost, data-efficiency proxy, regret to oracle, paired seed differences, stress sweeps, ablations, and failure cases.

\section{Failure cases}
The failure-case CSV records eight important limitations. The atlas still struggles when hidden soft-material deformation changes contact without observable boundary evidence, when the language or task goal changes, when sensor dropout hides an impact, when compliant fixtures break rigid-contact assumptions, when friction memory aliases visually identical states, when diagnostic probes add latency, when local decisions create long-horizon contact cascades, and when the oracle contact-mode supervisor reveals remaining headroom. These failures are useful because they narrow the next empirical version: it needs tactile/deformable-state estimation, instruction grounding, sensor-health modeling, latency-aware probing, long-horizon recovery, and external validation.

\section{Hostile prior-work boundary}
The paper's broad neighborhood is crowded. Non-smooth contact dynamics, implicit time stepping, complementarity contact models, MuJoCo-style simulation, contact dynamics learning, diffusion policies, and robotics transformers all pressure the contribution \citep{moreau1999nonsmooth,stewart1996implicit,anitescu1997formulating,todorov2012mujoco,pfrommer2020contactnets,chi2023diffusion,brohan2022rt1,brohan2023rt2}. The defensible boundary is therefore narrow: we do not claim a new contact solver or superiority over trained external foundation policies. We claim that an explicit contact-mode boundary audit is locally useful for preventing smooth policy interpolation across non-smooth mechanics events.

\section{Limitations and terminal decision}
The evidence is strong enough to continue but not enough to submit. The limitations are material:
\begin{itemize}
\item the benchmark is local and generated;
\item no real robot or independent high-fidelity simulator has been used;
\item the diffusion and transformer policies are surrogates, not external trained checkpoints;
\item there are no hardware videos, qualitative rollouts, or deployment logs;
\item the oracle gap remains nontrivial.
\end{itemize}

The terminal decision for this v4.1 continuation audit is \textbf{STRONG\_REVISE}. The next honest version must reproduce the contact-mode boundary advantage on real robots or accepted high-fidelity benchmarks with trained policy baselines. Without that evidence, the paper should not be submitted to ICLR main.
